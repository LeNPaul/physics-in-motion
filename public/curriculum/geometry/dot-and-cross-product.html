Dot and Cross Product

There are two kinds of vector multiplication: scalar product (also called the dot product) and vector product (also called the cross product).

Dot Product

The dot product is defined as the following:

$$ \vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = A B \cos \phi$$

Where \(\phi\) is the angle between \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\), and it is between \(0^\circ\) and \(180^\circ\). \(A\) and \(B\) are the magnitudes of \(\vec{A}\) and \(\vec{B}\). The projection of \(\vec{B}\) along \(\vec{A}\) is \(B \cos \phi\), so we can think of \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\) as the product of the projection of \(\vec{B}\) on \(\vec{A}\) with the magnitude of \(A\).

Applying these ideas to the unit vectors \((\vec{i},\vec{j},\vec{k})\) yields the following:

$$\vec{i} \cdot \vec{i} = \vec{j} \cdot \vec{j} = \vec{k} \cdot \vec{k} = (1)(1) \cos 0^\circ = 1$$

$$\vec{i} \cdot \vec{j} = \vec{i} \cdot \vec{k} = \vec{j} \cdot \vec{k} = (1)(1)\cos 90^\circ = 0$$

This leads to the following useful expression for \(\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B}\):

$$\vec{A} \cdot \vec{B} = (A_x \vec{i} + A_y \vec{j} + A_z \vec{k}) \cdot (B_x \vec{i} + B_y \vec{j} + B_z \vec{k}) = A_x B_x + A_y B_y + A_z B_z$$
